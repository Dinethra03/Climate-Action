Small Island Developing States (SIDS)

Small Island Developing States (SIDS) are a group of small islands and coastal countries that face unique and particular development challenges due to their small size, remoteness, vulnerability to natural disasters, and exposure to external economic shocks. There are currently 38 SIDS recognized by the United Nations. These states are characterized by their small land areas, limited resources, and often fragile ecosystems.

SIDS are particularly vulnerable to climate change, rising sea levels, and extreme weather events, which threaten their economies, infrastructure, and the livelihoods of their populations. Additionally, their small size and limited economies of scale often make it difficult for them to achieve sustainable development goals and to access international markets on favorable terms.

Despite these challenges, SIDS are also known for their rich cultural heritage, biodiversity, and unique ecosystems, which contribute to their resilience and provide opportunities for sustainable development through tourism, fisheries, and renewable energy.

The international community recognizes the special circumstances and challenges faced by SIDS and has developed several initiatives, including the Barbados Programme of Action and the Mauritius Strategy, to support their sustainable development and resilience-building efforts.
